How WebSWR works

WebSWR tests whether a retirement portfolio would have lasted, using real market history (1871–2025) and Monte Carlo simulation. Everything runs in your browser — no account, no tracking.

The basics

  1. Enter your portfolio value, how many years it must last, and your asset mix (must total 100%).
  2. Pick a spending strategy and amount.
  3. Click Run simulation.

The big number is your success rate — the share of historical retirements (or Monte Carlo trials) in which the money lasted the whole period. Green ≥ 95%, amber ≥ 75%, red below.

Historical vs Monte Carlo

  • Historical replays every actual sequence of returns since 1871 — a 30-year plan is tested starting in 1871, 1872, … 1996.
  • Monte Carlo builds thousands of synthetic futures: Bootstrap draws random past years; Block bootstrap draws runs of consecutive years (keeps bad streaks intact); Parametric samples a normal distribution fit to history. Bootstrap is usually the toughest test.

A caveat on the success rate. Historical cycles overlap — a 30-year window starting in 1965 shares 29 of its years with the 1966 one — so 126 cycles reflect only a handful of truly independent eras, and the failures cluster around a few bad episodes (1906, 1929, the late-1960s stagflation). Treat the number as a stress-test against everything markets have actually done, not as a probability with decimal-point precision.

Withdrawal strategies

  • Constant dollar (the “4% rule”) — a fixed amount, raised each year for inflation.
  • Percentage of portfolio — a fixed % of the current balance each year; it can never run dry, but your income swings with the market. Its Withdrawal frequency toggle chooses how the year's money is paid out: Annual takes it as one January lump; Monthly parks the year's withdrawal in a risk-free 3-month T-bill account and draws it down monthly, crediting the interest back to the portfolio at year-end (historical T-bill rates; 0% before 1928, when the series begins).
  • CAPE-based (Big ERN) — a valuation-aware percentage: each year you withdraw a + b × (1/CAPE) of the balance, so the rate is lower when stocks are expensive (high CAPE) and higher when they're cheap. See below.
  • Guyton-Klinger guardrails — constant-dollar with rules that trim spending after bad years and raise it after good ones.
  • VPW (variable percentage) — intentionally spends the portfolio down to ~$0 by the end, like a self-managed annuity.

Real vs Nominal

The balance chart can show today’s dollars (real) or face value (nominal). Real is the honest view — $1M in 50 years buys far less than today.

CAPE-based withdrawals (Big ERN)

The CAPE (cyclically-adjusted P/E, or “Shiller PE”) compares today’s price to the last 10 years of real earnings — a rough gauge of how expensive stocks are. Big ERN’s dynamic rule sets each year’s withdrawal rate to a + b × (1/CAPE), where 1/CAPE is the earnings yield. Defaults a = 1.75% and b = 0.5 (his toolbox settings): at CAPE 30 that’s 1.75% + 0.5×3.33% ≈ 3.4%; at a cheap CAPE 15 it rises to ≈ 5.1%. You spend less when valuations are stretched and more when they’re not, which historically avoids the worst sequence-of-returns busts.

Historical backtests use each year’s actual Shiller CAPE as of the start of that year (January — the value a retiree could really have acted on; validated against multpl.com). Monte Carlo starts from the current CAPE and evolves it forward with the simulated returns. That’s why the “first year” of a historical run is a range — every start year had its own valuation — and won’t equal the today’s-CAPE readout under the inputs, which is what year 1 looks like if you retire now.

The “better CAPE.” The headline CAPE runs permanently high partly because of buybacks and old tax rates. Following ERN’s “better CAPE”, WebSWR’s current CAPE grosses recent earnings up by the retained-earnings yield and normalizes them to today’s corporate tax rate — a lower, fairer number that supports a slightly higher withdrawal rate today. It reproduces ERN’s published adjusted CAPE to within a fraction of a point.

Kept current automatically. Nothing is fetched at runtime (that’s the whole security model). Instead, a daily server-side job re-runs the data pipeline and redeploys, so the shipped CAPE tracks the latest value on its own — there’s nothing to enter or update by hand.

Find max spending

Enter a target success rate and click the red button. WebSWR searches for the highest constant spending that still hits that target — against history or Monte Carlo. The answer appears under the button and is applied to your plan.

Will your money outlast you?

Set your age and sex, and the “Rich, broke, or dead?” chart layers SSA life-table survival odds onto your simulation: for each year it shows the chance you’re alive with money, alive but broke, or deceased. Often you’re far more likely to pass away with money left over than to run out of it.

Loan calculator

The Loan calc tab (top right) amortizes any fixed-rate loan — mortgage, car, student loan. It shows the monthly payment, total interest, payoff time, and how extra monthly principal shortens the loan and cuts interest.

Compound interest

The Compound tab grows a lump sum plus regular additions: pick the rate, how many times per year it compounds, and whether additions land at the start or end of each compounding period (the annual addition is spread evenly across those periods). You get the future value, total contributed vs. interest earned, and a year-by-year breakdown. Values are nominal — inflation and taxes are not applied.

Income & adjustments

Add Social Security, pensions, or one-off expenses. Years count from retirement (year 1 = your first year retired). Tick COLA to grow an amount with inflation.

Where the data comes from

Annual total returns: 1928–present from Aswath Damodaran (NYU Stern) with CPI-U inflation from the BLS; 1871–1927 reconstructed from Robert Shiller’s data (stocks, bonds, inflation only — an annual approximation). Gold, cash, corporate bonds, real estate and small-cap begin in 1928, so using them limits cycles to 1928+.

Privacy & safety

No accounts, no analytics, and the app sends nothing you enter anywhere — every simulation runs locally and your inputs never leave your browser. (The hosting CDN runs a standard bot-detection check for security; it never sees your numbers.) Copy link encodes your scenario into the URL so you can bookmark or share it; that link contains your numbers, so share it deliberately.
